Ayevat designed the social stratification. Ostentati demanded aesthetic perfection at every scale. Gel engineered the light. Yacob wrote the protocols. Mevertrase crafted the narrative.

    Albiron's Rise (~2526)¶

    Albiron claimed Gel's seat in ~2526, arriving with the earned contempt of a man who had profited from the Convergence War. The founding consortium had been idealists building a utopia. Albiron was a pragmatist who had made his fortune distributing weapons. His arrival forced Denebula to confront a question it had been avoiding: was the utopia built on ideals, or on money?

    The Planet: Denebula¶

    Galactic Monaco¶

    Denebula is the galaxy's zenith of desirability â€” the single most exclusive address in all of Galia. It is not the largest economy (that's Exodus, MUD's capital). It is not the most populous. It is the most concentrated â€” extreme per capita wealth in a setting designed for perfection.

    The comparison to Monaco is deliberate: a tiny, beautiful territory where the ultra-wealthy live in proximity, generating financial gravity that pulls markets across two dozen sectors into its orbit. The surrounding sector thrums with colonies and markets feeding off Denebula's prosperity. Adventurers and opportunists flock to the sector, drawn like moths to its golden allure.

Human, Sogmian, Ustur, Mierese, Punaab â€” all are reshaped to meet Denebula's aesthetic standards.

    The Caste System¶

    Society on Denebula is meticulously stratified into rigid castes, each with:

    • Intricate etiquette rules â€” social protocols that outsiders find incomprehensible but residents treat as sacred law
    • Ascending requirements â€” moving up requires massive ATLAS reserves at each tier

    The system is understood by all residents as the price of paradise. Those who cannot accept it are free to leave. Most do not.

    The Worker Planets¶

    Workers do not reside on Denebula itself. The planet is reserved exclusively for the elite and visiting wealthy. After their shifts â€” maintaining the gardens, serving in the establishments, operating the infrastructure â€” workers must retreat to neighboring planets designed exclusively as worker housing.

    The contrast between the paradise of Denebula and the functional dormitory worlds orbiting it is exactly as stark as it sounds. The workers accept the arrangement because Denebula wages are the highest in the MRZ, and because the alternative is unemployment in a galaxy that offers few guarantees.

    The Shadow Games¶

    In moments of ennui, the Merchant Princes engage in clandestine power plays â€” espionage, sabotage, market manipulation, and intricate political maneuvering.     The Rules (Unspoken)¶

    1. The games remain concealed. No Shadow Game operation may be publicly traceable to a Merchant Prince. Deniability is absolute.
    2. Denebula's reputation is sacrosanct. No game may damage the sector's image, economy, or infrastructure. The games are played outside Denebula, using pawns and proxies across the galaxy.
    3. No permanent elimination. A Prince may be financially weakened, politically embarrassed, or strategically outmaneuvered â€” but killed? That crosses a line. (Gel's death may or may not have crossed it.)
    4. Victory is its own reward. There is no prize for winning a Shadow Game except the knowledge that you've outplayed a peer. For beings who already own everything, this is enough.

    The Paradox¶

    The Shadow Games make Denebula simultaneously the safest and most dangerous place in the galaxy. The planet itself is a sanctuary â€” protected, beautiful, serene.
